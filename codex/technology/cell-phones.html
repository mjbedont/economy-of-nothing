Backstory

After the corporate success of brain implants as communication tools, public backlash and unforeseen side effects led corporations to innovate further. The next stage in evolution was the creation of **Cell Phones**, devices made entirely from organic matter. These phones are grown from synthetic cells, giving them the ability to self-repair minor damage and adapt to varying environmental conditions.

The organic nature of these phones allows for a deeper integration with their users, offering real-time biofeedback and customizable interfaces that can literally grow to meet the userâ€™s needs. However, their dependency on genetic engineering and limited network coverage has made them yet another tool of corporate control.

Key Features

  • Organic Composition: Built from synthetic cells that mimic biological processes.
  • Self-Healing: Minor scratches and damage are repaired over time by cellular regeneration.
  • Adaptive Signal Reception: Grows additional "signal nodes" for stronger reception in weak coverage areas.
  • Corporate Networks: Coverage varies significantly by region, often limited to corporate-owned zones.

Role in the Universe

Cell Phones in The Economy of Nothing highlight the extremes of corporate innovation and control. While they offer revolutionary features like self-healing casings and organic adaptability, their reliance on proprietary ecosystems ensures users remain tethered to corporate networks. Coverage limitations and high maintenance fees make them both a marvel of technology and a constant reminder of corporate overreach.
